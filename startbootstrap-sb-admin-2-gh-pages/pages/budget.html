So, you got a job, you got a sick new (one bedroom) appartment in a only slightly sketchy neighborhood, and you are donig as great as a disillusioned millenial/gen-z-er could be doing. Congrats!

And then, April comes. And in the wise words of Notorious B.I.G., "Mo Money, Mo Problems."

Welcome to tax season.

Let's take a deep breath. Theres a lot of language going on here. Lets break it down.

Ever notice that when you get your paycheck, you aren't getting all the money you are suppose to? Some of that money just.. floats away? Thats called Witholding Allowances and it basically means your employer takes some of your paycheck to pay the IRS. This has something to do with a W-4 Form, but assuming you already have an employer and a job, we won't tackle what this actually means today.

When you pay taxes, you aren't paying taxes on the actual amount of money your company paid you. You can subtract certain deductions from your income to create a Adjusted Gross Income. Good news! Student loan interest payments are deductable. Taxable Income is the income leftover after you subtract the exemptions and itemized deductions.

How about a tax bracket? What's that? Well, the US utilizes a graduated income tax system, meaning the more you mke, the more you pay. Tax brackets are income levels that are designated by the IRS and determine the tax rate for those incomes.

Tax Bracket Info

Now, lets make it just a little more confusing. Enter: Marginal Tax Rate. This makes calculating taxes a little more difficult. Marginal tax rate is the percentage of taxes you pay on your income based on all the tax brackets you qualify for. Let's take an example. Let's say A, B, and C are tax brackets, and you happen to fall into tax bracket C. Therefore, you technically qualify for both A and B. Let's say the A tax Bracket stops at $10k yearly income, and B stops at $20k. The first $10k of your income would be taxed at the A tax rate, and the income from $10k-20k would be taxed at the B tax rate. Then, the rest of your income would be taxed at the C rate. Therefore, your income is taxed seperately in 3 different ways to create a Marginal tax rate. You're effective tax rate is the average of all the tax rates that apply to you income.

Now: excemptions. More specifically, tax exemptions. Tax exemptions can be personal or dependent. If you have no children and your parents don't claim you as a dependent, you can claim a personal exemption yourself, reducing your taxable income by a set amount. If you are married and jointly file taxes, you can claim a personal exemption for your significant other as well. Dependent exemptions are claims you can make for each person you support financially. This is typically children. There are limits on exemptions, and once you pass a certain income, you can no longer longer claim exemptions.
